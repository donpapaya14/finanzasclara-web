## What the FDCPA says about medical debt
The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) protects consumers from abusive debt collection practices, including those related to medical debt. According to the [Consumer Financial Protection Bureau](https://www.consumerfinance.gov), the FDCPA applies to medical debt, as clarified by the 2010 expansion that included medical bills. Specifically, sections **1692g** and **1692e** of the FDCPA apply to medical debt collection. As stated in [CFPB Bulletin 2013-07 (Medical Debt Collection)](https://www.consumerfinance.gov), medical debt collectors must comply with these provisions.

## The 30-day validation notice window
When a debt collector first contacts you about a medical debt, you have the right to request debt validation within **30 days**. This can be done by sending a letter to the collector, using template language such as: 'I am requesting validation of the debt you are attempting to collect. Please provide me with documentation that includes the original contract, the amount of the debt, and any payments made.' A sample USPS certified mail receipt can serve as proof of your request. As outlined in [FDCPA Section 809 (15 USC 1692g)](https://www.law.cornell.edu), this notice window is crucial for protecting your rights.

## Common FDCPA violations in medical collections
Medical debt collectors often commit FDCPA violations, such as balance billing errors, inflated charges, or failure to provide itemized bills. According to a **2016 JAMA study**, approximately **49%** of medical bills contain errors. These errors can lead to overpayment or incorrect debt collection. It is essential to review your medical bills carefully and dispute any errors or inaccuracies.

## Disputing with credit bureaus within 60 days
If a medical debt appears on your credit report, you can dispute it with the credit bureau within **60 days** using the special rule for medical collections under [FCRA Section 605](https://www.law.cornell.edu). Here are the steps to follow:
1. Obtain a copy of your credit report from the credit bureau.
2. Identify the medical debt you wish to dispute.
3. Write a dispute letter to the credit bureau, citing the error or inaccuracy.
4. Include supporting documentation, such as a payment receipt or a letter from the creditor.
5. Send the dispute letter via certified mail, with return receipt requested.
As stated in the [CFPB Medical Debt Credit Reporting Rule (2023)](https://www.consumerfinance.gov), this process can help you resolve errors on your credit report.
