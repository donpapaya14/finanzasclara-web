## Introduction to Compound Interest
The power of **compound interest** can be seen in a simple example: $10,000 invested over 10, 20, or 30 years at different interest rates. Using the [SEC Compound Interest Calculator 2023](https://www.sec.gov), we can calculate the exact account balances for each scenario.

## The $10,000 Test: 5% vs. 7% vs. 9% Returns
The following table shows the account balances at 5, 10, 20, and 30 years for each return rate:
| Return Rate | 5 Years | 10 Years | 20 Years | 30 Years |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 5% | $11,276 | $16,389 | $33,861 | $64,091 |
| 7% | $12,763 | $19,672 | $49,325 | $106,161 |
| 9% | $14,436 | $23,537 | $71,459 | $174,494 |
As seen in the table, a 2% difference in return rate can result in a significant difference in account balance over decades.

## The Hidden Multiplier Effect After Year 15
According to the [Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) 2022](https://fred.stlouisfed.org), the compounding effect accelerates after the 15-year mark. For example, $10,000 at 7% earns more in years 20-30 than years 0-20. This 'hockey stick' growth curve can be seen in the following chart:
| Years | Interest Earned |
| --- | --- |
| 0-10 | $6,672 |
| 10-20 | $13,445 |
| 20-30 | $36,789 |
This chart shows the accelerating effect of compounding over time.

## What If You Added $100/Month?
Using the [Investor.gov Compound Interest Tool](https://www.investor.gov), we can compare the effect of adding $100/month to the initial $10,000 investment. The following table shows the account balances at 30 years for each scenario:
| Scenario | Account Balance |
| --- | --- |
| $10,000 only | $76,122 |
| $10,000 + $100/month | $217,919 |
As seen in the table, adding $100/month can significantly increase the account balance over 30 years.

## Inflation's Silent Tax (And How to Beat It)
According to the [Bureau of Labor Statistics CPI Data 2024](https://www.bls.gov), inflation can erode the purchasing power of your investment. For example, $76,122 at 7% is actually $31,445 in today's dollars, assuming 3% inflation. To beat inflation, consider investing in **TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities)** or **dividend stocks**.

## Frequently Asked Questions
### What is compound interest?
Compound interest is the interest earned on both the principal amount and any accrued interest. According to [Investopedia](https://www.investopedia.com), compound interest can help your investment grow exponentially over time.
### How does inflation affect compound interest?
Inflation can erode the purchasing power of your investment. According to the [Bureau of Labor Statistics](https://www.bls.gov), a 3% inflation rate can reduce the purchasing power of your investment by 50% over 20 years.
### What is the best way to maximize compound interest?
The best way to maximize compound interest is to start early, be consistent, and take advantage of tax-advantaged accounts such as a Roth IRA. According to [The Little Book of Common Sense Investing by John Bogle](https://www.amazon.com/Little-Book-Common-Sense-Investing/dp/0470138137), a long-term investment strategy can help you maximize compound interest.
### Can I use compound interest to retire early?
Yes, compound interest can help you retire early. According to [Forbes](https://www.forbes.com), a consistent investment strategy can help you build wealth over time and achieve your retirement goals.
### How do I calculate compound interest?
You can calculate compound interest using a formula or a compound interest calculator. According to [Calculator Soup](https://www.calculatorsoup.com), the formula for compound interest is A = P (1 + r/n)^(nt), where A is the amount of money accumulated after n years, including interest, P is the principal amount, r is the annual interest rate (in decimal), n is the number of times that interest is compounded per year, and t is the time the money is invested for in years.
