## What is Dollar-cost Averaging?
Dollar-cost averaging is an **investing strategy** that involves investing a fixed amount of money at regular intervals, regardless of the market's performance. According to [Investopedia](https://www.investopedia.com/), this strategy helps reduce the impact of market volatility on investments. By investing a fixed amount of money at regular intervals, investors can **lower their average cost per share** over time.

## Real Historical Data: Dollar-cost Averaging Example
A study by [Yahoo Finance](https://finance.yahoo.com/) found that investing $10,000 in the S&P 500 index from 2010 to 2015 using dollar-cost averaging would have resulted in a **10.2% annual return**. This is compared to a **9.5% annual return** for a lump sum investment. The study used historical data to simulate the investment returns.

## Step-by-Step Setup for Dollar-cost Averaging
To set up a dollar-cost averaging investment plan, follow these steps:
1. Choose a **brokerage account** that offers low fees and a user-friendly interface.
2. Set up **automatic transfers** from your bank account to your brokerage account.
3. Select the **investment vehicle**, such as an index fund or ETF.
4. Determine the **investment amount** and **frequency**.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid with Dollar-cost Averaging
According to [The Balance](https://www.thebalance.com/), common pitfalls to avoid when using dollar-cost averaging include:
* **Market timing**: trying to time the market by investing more when the market is high and less when the market is low.
* **Not monitoring fees**: failing to monitor the fees associated with the investment vehicle.

## Tax Implications of Dollar-cost Averaging
The [IRS](https://www.irs.gov/) states that dollar-cost averaging can have tax implications. For example, if an investor sells a portion of their investment, they may be subject to **capital gains tax**. It is essential to consult with a tax professional to understand the tax implications of dollar-cost averaging.
