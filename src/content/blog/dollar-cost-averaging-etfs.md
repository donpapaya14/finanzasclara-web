## Introduction to Dollar-Cost Averaging
Dollar-cost averaging is an **investing strategy** that involves investing a fixed amount of money at regular intervals, regardless of the market's performance. This approach helps reduce **timing risks** and avoids emotional decisions based on market volatility, as noted by [Investopedia](https://www.investopedia.com). According to a study by the University of California, Berkeley (2020), dollar-cost averaging can reduce the impact of market volatility by up to **25%**.

## Choosing the Right ETFs for Dollar-Cost Averaging
When selecting **ETFs** for dollar-cost averaging, it's essential to consider **diversification**, **low fees**, and **tracking error**. A study by [Morningstar](https://www.morningstar.com) (2023) found that ETFs with low fees and high diversification tend to perform better in the long run. ## Step-by-Step Setup for Dollar-Cost Averaging with ETFs
To set up a dollar-cost averaging plan with ETFs, follow these steps:
1. Open a **brokerage account** with a reputable online broker, such as Fidelity or Vanguard.
2. Select the ETFs you want to invest in, based on your investment goals and risk tolerance.
3. Set up a regular investment plan, where you invest a fixed amount of money at regular intervals.
4. Automate your investments, so that the money is transferred from your bank account to your brokerage account automatically.

Historical data shows that dollar-cost averaging can be an effective way to reduce risk and increase returns. ## Managing and Adjusting Your Dollar-Cost Averaging Plan
To manage and adjust your dollar-cost averaging plan, it's essential to monitor your investments regularly and rebalance your portfolio as needed. According to [Charles Schwab](https://www.schwab.com) (2023), it's recommended to review your investment portfolio at least **once a year** and rebalance it if necessary.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid in Dollar-Cost Averaging with ETFs
There are several common mistakes to avoid when using dollar-cost averaging with ETFs, including:
1. Not having a **long-term perspective**, and trying to time the market.
2. Not **regularly reviewing** your investment portfolio.
3. Not **diversifying** your investments, and putting all your eggs in one basket.
