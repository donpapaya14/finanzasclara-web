## Introduction to Calculating Real APR
Calculating real APR on loans is crucial for understanding the true cost of borrowing. According to the [Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov), the **Annual Percentage Rate (APR)** is the interest rate charged on a loan over a year, including fees. For instance, a $1,000 loan with a 12% APR will cost $120 in interest over a year, making the total repayment $1,120.

## What is APR and How is it Calculated?
The APR is calculated by taking into account the loan amount, interest rate, and fees. For example, if you borrow $1,000 at an interest rate of 10% per annum, with a $50 origination fee, the APR would be **10.5%**. This is because the origination fee is spread over the loan term, increasing the effective interest rate. As explained by the [Federal Reserve (2022)](https://www.federalreserve.gov), understanding APR is vital for making informed borrowing decisions.

## Real APR vs. Advertised APR: The Difference
The real APR and advertised APR can differ significantly due to hidden fees and charges. According to the [Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (2019)](https://www.consumerfinance.gov), **70%** of consumers do not fully understand the terms of their loans, leading to unexpected costs. For instance, a loan with an advertised APR of 10% might have a real APR of **15%** when including late payment fees and other charges.

## Hidden Fees and Charges on Loans
Hidden fees on loans can significantly increase the real APR. Common hidden fees include **origination fees**, **late payment fees**, and **prepayment penalties**. According to the [National Foundation for Credit Counseling (2020)](https://www.nfcc.org), these fees can add up to **20%** of the loan amount. For example, a $1,000 loan with a 10% origination fee will require a repayment of $1,100, plus interest.

## Tips for Negotiating with Lenders
To get a better deal on your loan, it's essential to negotiate with lenders. According to [Credit Karma (2020)](https://www.creditkarma.com), **60%** of lenders are willing to negotiate loan terms. Here are some tips for negotiating with lenders:
1. **Check your credit score**: A good credit score can give you leverage to negotiate better loan terms.
2. **Compare loan offers**: Research and compare loan offers from different lenders to find the best deal.
3. **Ask about fees**: Clarify all fees associated with the loan, including origination fees and late payment fees.
